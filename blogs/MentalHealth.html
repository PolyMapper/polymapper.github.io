Mapping for Mental Health: Using GIS to Design Happier Cities

December 18, 2024 by Gordon McLachlan

Life in a busy city can be thrilling, but it also comes with challenges. From noisy streets to overcrowded spaces, urban living can take a toll on mental health. But what if we could design cities to help us feel calmer, more connected, and genuinely happier?

This is where GIS (Geographical Information Systems) comes into play. Far from being just a mapping tool, GIS is helping urban planners create environments that actively support mental well-being.

Why Mental Health Should Be Part of Urban Design

More than half the world’s population now lives in cities, and this number keeps climbing. While cities provide opportunities like jobs, culture, and education, they also bring challenges such as pollution, noise, overcrowding, and limited access to green spaces. These factors can have a serious impact on mental health.

The World Health Organization highlights mental health as a key part of overall well-being, and urban planners are starting to catch up. They’re working to design spaces that reduce stress, build community, and offer quiet moments of relaxation. GIS is proving to be an invaluable tool in making this a reality.

How GIS is Helping Create Healthier Cities

GIS offers urban planners a data-driven way to tackle issues like stress, social isolation, and access to nature. By mapping these challenges, it’s easier to identify where changes can make the most impact.

Bringing Green Spaces to the Forefront

Green spaces like parks and gardens are known to reduce stress and improve mental health. GIS can help identify areas with limited access to greenery, enabling planners to prioritise the creation of new parks or improve existing ones.

In London, for example, GIS revealed that people living within 300 metres of a park experienced lower levels of anxiety. This led to policy changes focusing on bringing green spaces to underserved areas.

Designing Cities for Walking

Walkable neighbourhoods are more than just practical—they’re also good for mental health. Walking boosts mood, reduces reliance on cars, and encourages social interaction. GIS allows planners to study pedestrian networks, highlight barriers such as unsafe crossings, and design better connections between key areas.

Copenhagen used GIS to map out pedestrian-friendly routes that link parks, residential areas, and cultural sites. These pathways not only encourage relaxation but also help residents engage with their surroundings in more meaningful ways.

Creating Spaces for Social Connection

Loneliness and social isolation are significant urban challenges. GIS can help by identifying where public spaces, such as plazas or community hubs, are needed most. By analysing data on population density and accessibility, planners can ensure these spaces meet the needs of the community.

Melbourne used this approach to create “social hubs” that combine libraries, cafes, and activity centres. Located strategically for maximum accessibility, these hubs have become lively gathering spots that help foster stronger community ties.

A Real-World Example: Toronto’s Mental Health Mapping

In Toronto, planners used GIS to examine how urban spaces were affecting mental health. Their analysis focused on three areas:

  • Green Space Access: Identifying areas where parks were out of reach.
  • Noise Pollution: Highlighting locations with high traffic noise linked to stress.
  • Community Facilities: Assessing the distribution of libraries, playgrounds, and community centres.

The insights led to targeted interventions. Unused lots were converted into pocket parks, noise barriers were installed along busy roads, and new community hubs were added to underserved neighbourhoods. These changes made a noticeable difference in residents’ quality of life.

Challenges in Using GIS for Mental Health

While GIS offers powerful solutions, it does come with challenges. Mental health is complex and deeply personal, making it hard to measure in straightforward terms. Collecting accurate data can be tricky, and planners must also ensure that interventions are inclusive, reaching all communities equitably.

Even with these obstacles, advances in technology and growing awareness of mental health are helping to overcome these barriers.

Looking Ahead: The Future of Mental Health Mapping

As cities expand, GIS will play an increasingly important role in designing spaces that support mental health. New technologies, like real-time data from wearable devices or AI-driven spatial analysis, could open up even more possibilities.

Imagine planners being able to predict stress hotspots by analysing crowd patterns or noise levels. Or creating live maps of well-being that adapt as cities change. With tools like these, mental health could become a key consideration in every aspect of urban design.

Building Cities That Care for Us

Cities should be more than places to live and work—they should be places where people can truly thrive. By combining GIS technology with a focus on mental health, we can create urban environments that offer calm, connection, and access to nature.

Whether it’s adding a park, improving walkways, or designing a vibrant community hub, GIS is helping us rethink what cities can be. The result? Happier, healthier urban spaces that benefit everyone.
