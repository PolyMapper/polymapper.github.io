The Analytical Hierarchy Process in GIS: Simplifying Site Selection

July 28, 2024 by Gordon McLachlan

Finding the right site for a hospital, infrastructure project, or conservation effort often means weighing competing priorities. Cost, accessibility, environmental concerns—how do you strike the right balance? This is where the Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) comes in. Paired with GIS, it offers a structured, data-driven way to tackle complex decisions, breaking them into smaller, manageable steps.

In this post, we’ll look at how AHP works in GIS, why the two tools complement each other so well, and how they’re applied in real-world planning.

What is AHP?

The Analytical Hierarchy Process, developed by Thomas Saaty, is a tool for multi-criteria decision-making. Its strength lies in its ability to simplify complex decisions by organising them into a hierarchy. Decision-makers then compare criteria systematically, assigning weights to reflect their importance. This approach not only improves clarity but also ensures decisions are more transparent and justifiable.

Why Combine AHP and GIS?

GIS adds a powerful spatial dimension to AHP, allowing planners to visualise and analyse geographic data alongside their criteria. Instead of simply ranking potential sites, GIS creates suitability maps that clearly show the best options.

  • Incorporate both spatial and non-spatial data.
  • Visualise the impact of different criteria.
  • Break down complicated problems into straightforward maps.

Step-by-Step: How AHP Works in GIS

1. Set Your Goal and Criteria: Define the objective and organise criteria into a hierarchy, such as accessibility, demographics, environment, and cost.

2. Pairwise Comparisons: Rank the importance of each criterion relative to others. Assign weights based on logical comparisons and calculate a consistency ratio.

3. Prepare Spatial Data: Represent each criterion as a GIS layer, such as population density or proximity to transport. Standardise the data for comparability.

4. Perform a Weighted Overlay: Combine the layers using the calculated weights to generate a suitability map highlighting optimal areas.

Real-World Example: Hospital Site Selection

A city council planning a hospital might prioritise:

  • Accessibility: Proximity to main roads and transport hubs.
  • Demographics: Areas with high population density.
  • Environment: Avoidance of flood zones and steep slopes.
  • Cost: Preference for lower land values.

Benefits of AHP in GIS

  • Simplifies complex problems, making them easier to manage.
  • Incorporates stakeholder input through pairwise comparisons.
  • Creates clear visuals like intuitive suitability maps.
  • Consistency and repeatability for defensible decisions.

Challenges to Keep in Mind

While effective, challenges include ensuring data quality, managing subjectivity in comparisons, and addressing time constraints for preparation and analysis.

Applications Beyond Site Selection

AHP and GIS are widely used in disaster management, urban planning, and renewable energy projects. For instance, identifying evacuation routes, balancing development with conservation, or siting wind farms all benefit from this approach.

Final Thoughts

The Analytical Hierarchy Process is more than a decision-making tool—it’s a way to bring clarity to complex projects. When paired with GIS, it ensures decisions are systematic, transparent, and practical. Whether it’s urban growth or environmental trade-offs, AHP and GIS provide the clarity and precision needed to make informed choices.
