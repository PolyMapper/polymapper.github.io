Crowdsourced Data in Geospatial Projects: Pros, Cons, and Best Practices

January 8, 2025 by Gordon McLachlan

In the last two decades, crowdsourced geospatial data has transformed the way we map the world. From OpenStreetMap (OSM) to citizen science projects tracking environmental changes, the power of collective data gathering has enabled faster, cheaper, and often more detailed mapping than traditional methods. But while crowdsourcing has opened new frontiers, it comes with challenges—issues of accuracy, bias, and reliability that can’t be ignored.

So, how do we harness the power of crowdsourced data while managing its pitfalls? Let’s explore the impact of crowdsourced geospatial data, its strengths and weaknesses, and best practices for ensuring its quality and usability.

The Rise of Crowdsourced Geospatial Data

Crowdsourcing geospatial data isn’t a new concept. Long before the internet, amateur cartographers contributed to local mapping efforts, and community-driven surveys helped track land use. But digital tools, smartphones, and web-based platforms have taken it to a new level.

The Pros of Crowdsourced Geospatial Data

Speed and Scalability: Traditional mapping projects can take years, requiring large teams and expensive resources. Crowdsourcing enables near-instant data collection on a global scale.

Cost-Effectiveness: Hiring field surveyors or using commercial satellite data is expensive. Crowdsourced data, often collected by volunteers, dramatically reduces costs while still providing valuable insights.

Local Knowledge and Remote Coverage: Many official datasets overlook rural or under-mapped areas. Crowdsourcing leverages local expertise, allowing people to map their own communities.

The Cons and Challenges of Crowdsourced Data

Accuracy and Reliability Issues: Unlike official geospatial datasets, crowdsourced data isn’t always vetted by professionals. Contributors might unintentionally add incorrect locations or misclassify features.

Bias and Inconsistency: Crowdsourced data reflects the interests and biases of its contributors. Urban areas with active digital communities often get detailed mapping, while rural areas may be left out.

Data Ownership and Privacy Concerns: Many platforms are open-source, but others, like Google Maps, benefit commercially from unpaid contributions.

Best Practices for Using Crowdsourced Geospatial Data

Implement Quality Control Measures: Validation methods such as peer review, AI-assisted checks, and cross-referencing with authoritative sources can improve data reliability.

Encourage Diverse Participation: Seeking contributions from underrepresented regions can help reduce mapping bias.

Use Hybrid Approaches: Many organisations combine official datasets with crowdsourced contributions to ensure balance between speed and accuracy.

The Future of Crowdsourced Geospatial Data

With advancements in AI, automation, and blockchain technology, the future of crowdsourced geospatial data looks promising. AI can assist in data validation, ensuring accuracy without requiring human intervention, while blockchain could enhance data transparency and ownership.

When used wisely—with proper validation, ethical safeguards, and community engagement—crowdsourced data is a powerful tool for building a more informed, connected, and mapped world.
