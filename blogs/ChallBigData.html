The Challenges of Big Data in GIS

December 9, 2018 by Gordon McLachlan

In an increasingly digital world available geospatial data is growing exponentially, with more accurate and numerous remote sensing systems, earth monitoring and location enabled devices, than ever before (Figures 1 and 2). Due to increasing size, diversity, and complexity of geospatial data, it is commonly regarded as Big Data. A universal definition of Big Data is elusive, however, it can be characterised by large volume, high velocity, extreme variety, and low veracity – the four Vs.

Geospatial Big Data can be split into two categories, physical and human. Physical geospatial Big Data, such remote sensing, is often used within the earth sciences, providing greater insight into highly spatio-temporal events such as deforestation or glacial retreat. Human geospatial Big Data maps societal interactions within space. Analysis of such Big Data will allow detailed insight into the patterns of society, enabling more spatially focused policies by decision makers.

The unprecedented volumes of geospatial Big Data, the rapid speed at which it is created, and its plethora of sources and heterogeneous nature, all challenge the fundamental way in which GIS stores, interacts and analyses data. Typical GIS work from a centralised data source, commonly through relational database management systems (RDBMS). However, RDBMS have been found increasingly inadequate in meeting the complex and dynamic storage requirements of Big Data, and the necessity to deal with unstructured data. The large volumes also increase the processing power needed to analyse the data, often surpassing standard computer capability. Furthermore, increasing demand for data streaming and real-time analysis, requires a paradigm shift from static geospatial data to a more inherently dynamic source. The need to accommodate dynamic observations is a fundamental consideration for any future GIS.

Challenges to GIS from Big Data do not only come from the data itself but also from interpretation and presentation. While Big Data can be unreliable, due to the human element collected from social media, or faulty earth monitoring devices in harsh condition, the most significant uncertainty can come from the sheer number of records. Analysis can produce false correlations and endogeneity, undermining the validity of statistical results.

The visualisation of Big Data is a challenge to GIS that is often underestimated. GIS faces the task of presenting multi-dimensional Big Data in a way easily interpreted by decision makers. Navtivi et al (2015) suggests that geospatial big data visualisation should allow the user to manipulate the presentation, enabling ad hoc filtering and querying. The successful representation of Big Data can reveal hidden patterns, adding a fifth V to the defining characteristics of Big Data – Value.

Geospatial Big Data has a number of applications and huge potential value, but the challenges of volume, velocity, variety, veracity, and visualisation all need to be overcome. However, there are some options available to help GIS best utilise geospatial Big Data, including: NoSQL databases, cloud computing, and parallel processing. Combined they allow for flexibility, highly scalable, fault tolerance and distributive computing infrastructures, although these options have their own challenges such as privacy and security. Whilst these go a long way in integrating GIS with Big Data there is still much research to be done, especially with a way of visualising the new multidimensional data.
