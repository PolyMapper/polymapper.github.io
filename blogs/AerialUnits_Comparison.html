Areal unit or surface data: Which is better for representing population?

December 2, 2019 by Gordon McLachlan

Public health, emergency planning, land use, and other socioeconomic sectors all use spatial analytical functions, such as spatial interaction modelling and catchment creation. To fully utilise the capability of these functions within a GIS, population data showing accurate spatial distribution is critical. Population data is often represented as either the widely used polygonal areal units, or as a raster continuous surface.

A census is a rich source of population data. Privacy constraints mean census data is aggregated into areal units, such as Output Areas in the UK. The widespread use of areal units for population representation is partly owing to this release mechanism, but also due to its complete coverage and ability to be readily understood and explained. For example, Abbas et al. (2009) demonstrates the utility of geodemographics, based largely on census data and areal units, within the health care environment.

Although advantageous to use areal units for population representation, they do contain inherent analytical and theoretical issues. Areal units are often created by governments to suit administrative purposes, and frequently do not relate to natural population distributions. Altering areal units could profoundly impact results of spatial analysis, an affect known as the modifiable areal unit problem (MAUP). Populations within areal units are also assumed to be homogenously distributed, resulting in spatial variability being lost, leading to misleading analytical results. The ecological fallacy is illustrated by Martin (1989), where an undeveloped dockland area in Cardiff was grouped with a neighbouring, socially disadvantaged, area, thus implying the dockland had the same high percentage of unemployed (Figure 1), although it has a small population and large bodies of water.

The need for interzonal spatial distribution has driven the development of alternative methods of population representation. One such alternative is disaggregating areal unit data into a continuous surface through interpolation. Population surfaces can be created through relatively simple areal interpolation methods, such as population-weighted centroids, and areal weighting. They can also be created through more complex dasysmetric mapping methods, utilising ancillary data to make more detailed population estimates.

There are many advantages to using a well-constructed population surface. Due to the continuous nature of surface data, they are not constrained by arbitrary boundaries, thus, allowing more detailed spatial distribution of populations and more accurate maps. Moreover, Minnis (2003) stated that population surfaces can be aggregated to most areal units, consequently decreasing MAUP. However, population will still be homogenous within each grid square, but to what extent will depend on resolution. An additional advantage is the ability to compare, overlay, and buffer, two or more population datasets with non-coincidental boundaries. This permits population data to be assessed temporally from census to census, regardless of areal unit change (Figure 2). It also enables disparate sources to be pulled together to aid analysis, e.g. health care provision or crime prevention.

While areal units are a common, and easy way to represent population data, they can be misleading. One way to circumvent these issues is to create a population surface. It must, however, be noted that population surfaces are just estimates, and quality is largely dependent on the source data and resolution. Each surface creation method has its own merits and drawbacks, with each making its own inherent assumptions on the relationship between ancillary and population or the use of distance decay, but that goes beyond the scope of this paper.
