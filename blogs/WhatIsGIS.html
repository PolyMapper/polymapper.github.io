What is GIS?

November 12, 2018 by Gordon McLachlan

Answering the question, “What is GIS?” is as broad as asking, “What is geography?”. The answer for both depends on your context, your aim, and your background. At a fundamental level GIS stands for Geographical Information System, which is a system that is specifically designed to capture, manage, manipulate, analyse, and visualise spatial data. Such systems include the widely renowned ArcGIS, MapInfo, and the open source offering QGIS. With GIS technology, people can compare the locations of different things in order to discover how they relate to each other. For example, using GIS, a single map could include sites that produce pollution, such as factories, and sites that are sensitive to pollution, such as wetlands and rivers. Such a map would help people determine where water supplies are most at risk.

I would argue that the discipline of GIS goes beyond the meaning of software, and moves into the academic realm of spatial analysis in general, as well as specialist knowledge of the software. Merely having the tools, i.e. the Geographical Information System, does not enable the user to produce highly insightful spatial analysis – a deeper understanding of the geographical theory behind the plethora of functions available in modern GIS is required to get the very most out of the GIS. This would be similar for disciplines such as computing or programming; the connotation behind the term goes beyond the literal meaning. In addition, it encompasses the understanding of the spatial data itself: what type of spatial data can be used for which tasks, and how can such data be collected.

GIS a fascinating field because, again like geography, it can be related to almost everything. Most things that occur will happen in place. That is to say, locational data is becoming a very important part of our lives, even if we don’t know it yet. It is becoming a big part of Big Data, which we are all giving off constantly. Mentioning Big Data opens a whole other world of complexity and veracity, therefore, it will be discussed in a latter blog.

In summary, GIS has duel meanings, it is both the software used to collect, analyse and represent spatial data, but it is also the discipline of using such software to collect, analyse and represent spatial data. Although there may appear to be no distinction, once you work with a GIS, you will realise there is.
